The esteem and potential of the Reggio approach: Improving learning and educating

1. The emergent curriculum and a project-based approach:


Teachers use an emergent curriculum to support project work that allows students to dig deeper into their areas of interest. Children are encouraged to take charge of their education and pursue their interests. A project-based approach to learning experiences emphasizes the significance of 'learning by doing’ (Arseven, 2014)

2. Collaboration and Relationship:

Learning is considered a dynamic, social co construction process built on interactions and connections at Reggio Emilia.

3. The Child's Image:

Teachers are aware of each child's potential and are responsible for assisting and guiding them throughout their educational journey (Arseven, 2014).

4. A Holistic Approach: Creativity:

Children are supported and encouraged to find and experience a variety of methods to communicate their thoughts, ideas, and creativity through their "hundred languages" since creativity is at the heart of the Reggio approach (Arseven, 2019).

5. The learning environment as a third teacher:

Open, inviting settings with natural furniture, real-life materials, mirrors, windows, and natural lighting characterise Reggio-inspired classrooms (Robson, 2016).

6. Documentation:

Educators exhibit a 'pedagogy of listening' to children and ways of valuing and supporting them as people as they make sense of their environment by watching, interpreting, and recording their learning process (Arseven, 2019)

7. Learning dispositions and skills:

Children are also given the adaptability to investigate their interests, make choices, and alter the course of their learning, which helps them handle risk and responsibility and develop arranging abilities (Arseven, 2014).
